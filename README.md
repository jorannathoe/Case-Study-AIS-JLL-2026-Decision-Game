To explore how tensions identified in (web) archival literature, practice-based observations and interviews translate into institutional decision-making, we developed an interactive simulation tool titled: Making the Archive: A Web Archiving Decision Simulator. 
Rather than proposing a single strategy for web archiving, this tool was designed to model how different institutional priorities can lead to divergent archival outcomes. 

In doing so, it functions as a reflective tool that allows users to experiment with different decision pathways and consider the consequences of their choices. 
The design is informed by a literature review, interviews and stakeholder mapping exercises we performed during our case study. 

Taken together, these sources helped identify recurring decision points that institutions encounter when developing their web archiving practices. These decision points were subsequently translated into a structured set of dilemmas.
Each dilemma offers up two possible choices, where discrete decisions influence a set of archival values. Each pathway consists of four sequential choices, all generating different outcomes that illustrate how different institutional priorities influence what sort of collection is ultimately created. 
The decisions remain constant for each pathway. 

The first dilemma is concerned with setting the scope of what will be collected in the web archive. 
Users decide between aligning themselves with the prevailing institutional guidelines of gathering news media, or branching out and gathering sources from social media as well. 
This choice reflects the challenges institutions face with one the hand wanting to be ambitious in their methodology and introduce representational variety, while also being able to sustainably manage their archival environment. 

Afterwards, users are faced with how they wish to collect the resources within that specified scope. 
This dilemma navigates between privileging automated technologies and human-led collection strategies. While automated web crawling might offer cost-effective and comprehensive capture, they are less adaptable to version changes and the institution is faced with the opacity of black-box algorithms. 
Meanwhile, the humanistic approach means getting to grips with new workflows and navigating blind spots and potential biases through data envelopes. On the other hand, this approach provides more curatorial selectivity and adaptability. 

The third dilemma focuses on collection expansion. Here, the user is faced with the choice to either build inter-organizational heritage infrastructures through linked open data or further contextualizing their collection through community-driven participatory initiatives. 
This decision node thereby explores what forms of knowledge production are privileged when expanding their infrastructure. 

Finally, players arrive at the conclusive dilemma, which explores two different access regimes for the web archive. There is the choice to make the collection freely available online, or to make it accessible through a request system whereby users motivate their reasons for its consultation. 

Each decision is tied to a scoring system that operationalizes five institutional values, namely: ethics, sustainability, legality, transparency and completeness. 
Making decisions therefore influences how the collection and institution is regarded in terms of these value dimensions. Thereby the player is directly confronted with the tradeoffs that different decisions consist of and how these lead to divergent archival outcomes. 
These outcomes are consequently visualized into sixteen spider graphs that model what institutional priorities were emphasized during the playthrough. 
The simulator thereby translates abstract debates about web archiving into concrete scenarios that allow institutions to reflect on how different priorities shape the archives they aim to produce. 

The simulator nevertheless has its limitations. For instance, there are a lot more dilemmas left to explore and existing dilemmas could be expanding through the addition of more choices, such as a hybrid collection strategy. 
Additionally, the current model does not simulate interdependencies between different choices, for example, how a broad scope may constrain accessibility choices due to copyright and ethical concerns. 
Furthermore, the value system is heavily dependent upon our interpretation of web archiving literature as well as our practical understanding drawing only on one institutional case study site.  
As a consequence, we elected to include a set of reflexive questions at each end scenario, where we explicitly ask for feedback on the tool and on unexplored strategies. We hope that by making the files freely available, interested parties will be inspired to further develop the simulator to suit their needs.

Ultimately, our aim was to design a reflective tool that supports institutional discussions around their web archiving strategy. 
It has been tested at the NIOD, where we discussed our report and played through the simulator, letting different employees argue their preferred choices before collectively making final decisions. 
Thereby, the simulator externalizes decision-making into an interactive format, which provides a shared language through which different perspectives and priorities can be articulated more clearly. 
Used in internal meetings or in workshop settings, the simulator may thus help reduce uncertainty in strategic discussions by translating abstract tensions into a structured set of choices and outcomes for other heritage institutions.
